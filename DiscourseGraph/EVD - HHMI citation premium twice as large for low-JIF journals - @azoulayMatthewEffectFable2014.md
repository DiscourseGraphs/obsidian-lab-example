After HHMI appointment, the citation premium accruing to pre-existing articles is approximately twice as large for articles published in low-impact-factor journals as for articles in high-impact-factor journals.

## Description

> "the HHMI citation premium is approximately twice as large for articles appearing in less prestigious journals." ([[Azoulay et al. - 2014 - Matthew Effect or Fable.pdf#page=25|p. 25]])

This finding is consistent with status theory: when a journal's prestige already serves as a strong signal of article quality (high JIF), additional author status provides less incremental information to readers. Conversely, where journal quality is a weak signal (low JIF), readers resort more heavily to author status as a quality heuristic — inflating citations for high-status authors in exactly the venues where quality signal is most needed.

## Methods Context

### What?
Journal Impact Factor (JIF) used as a proxy for article-level uncertainty/quality signal strength. Articles split on JIF as a moderator of the treatment effect.

### How?
Interaction term between HHMI appointment indicator and JIF-based journal prestige category. Table 3, Column 2 in the paper.

### Who?
Same HHMI-ECPW matched sample. Analysis restricted to pre-appointment articles.

## Other Notes
- The logic implies that citation counts are most distorted by author status in exactly the venues that lack other quality markers — a compounding problem for bibliometric assessment.
- This finding supports the interpretation that status serves as a *substitute* quality signal, not an independent boost to citations.
