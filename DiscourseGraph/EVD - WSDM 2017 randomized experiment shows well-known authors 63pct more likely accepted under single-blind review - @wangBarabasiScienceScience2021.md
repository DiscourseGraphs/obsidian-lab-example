A randomized experiment at the 2017 WSDM computer science conference found that well-known authors were 63% more likely to have papers accepted under single-blind than double-blind review; top-university authors were 58% more likely; authors from Google, Facebook, or Microsoft were 110% more likely.

## Description

> "Well-known author—defined as having at least three papers accepted by previous WSDM conferences and at least 100 computer science papers in total—were 63% more likely to have the paper accepted under single-blind review than in double-blind review... authors from top universities had a 58% increase in acceptance once their affiliation was known... for authors working at Google, Facebook or Microsoft... the acceptance rate more than doubled, increasing by 110%." ([[Wang & Barabási - 2021 - The Science of Science|p. ]])

This is an upstream causal intervention with direct consequences for citation accumulation: published papers can be cited; rejected papers cannot. Author identity and institutional prestige, when visible to reviewers, cause acceptance rates to more than double for some groups. The citation confound therefore begins at the publication stage — the citation record is not a random sample of submitted science but one filtered through a status-sensitive acceptance process. `[~T]`

## Methods Context

### What?
Randomized controlled experiment at the 2017 ACM WSDM (Web Search and Data Mining) conference, comparing acceptance rates under single-blind (reviewers know author identity) vs double-blind (author identity masked) review conditions.

### How?
Random assignment of submitted papers to review tracks; acceptance rates compared conditional on author characteristics (fame, institutional affiliation, industry affiliation).

### Who?
Submissions to WSDM 2017; well-known authors defined as ≥3 prior WSDM acceptances and ≥100 CS publications; top universities and major tech companies as institutional categories.

## Other Notes
- This is the strongest experimental evidence in the corpus for the Matthew effect operating upstream of citations: a true randomized design showing that author identity *causes* differential acceptance, not merely correlates with it.
- The 110% acceptance boost for industry-affiliated (Google/Facebook/Microsoft) authors is particularly striking — institutional status from industry, not just academic pedigree, drives differential publication access.
- Double-blind review largely eliminates the gaps, showing that the process is fixable at the review stage; the question is how much legacy citation advantage has already been generated by years of single-blind review.
- Single conference, single field — generalizability to all science is uncertain, but directionally consistent with the broader evidence base on status effects in evaluation.
