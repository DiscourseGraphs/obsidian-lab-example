## Grounding
> "the findings support the idea that status is a social cue that conveys the greatest information to audiences when there is uncertainty about product quality" (Azoulay et al. 2014, Introduction)

The mechanism underlying citation inflation by status is not uniform — it is concentrated precisely where it is hardest to defend against: novel work, cross-disciplinary work, and work published in lower-prestige venues. Where the scientific community has accumulated dense citation evidence and domain expertise, author status matters less; where evidence is thin and audiences are outside the focal field, readers fall back on reputation as a quality heuristic. This means citation confounding is largest for exactly the work that is most difficult to evaluate by any other means.

**Supporting evidence:**
- [[EVD - HHMI citation premium twice as large for low-JIF journals - @azoulayMatthewEffectFable2014]] — lower journal prestige signals quality uncertainty; readers rely more on author status
- [[EVD - HHMI citation boost concentrated on novel and cross-disciplinary articles - @azoulayMatthewEffectFable2014]] — novelty and interdisciplinarity produce uncertain quality signals
- [[EVD - HHMI treatment effect larger for high-recombinant-scope articles and zero for proximate antecedents - @azoulayMatthewEffectFable2014]] — cross-field prior art makes work harder to evaluate for most readers
- [[EVD - Less eminent HHMI investigators experience larger post-appointment citation premium - @azoulayMatthewEffectFable2014]] — when the scientist's own reputation is less established, the status shock conveys more new information

**Key caveats:**
- The "uncertainty" mechanism is inferred from heterogeneous treatment effects, not directly measured — the claim is an interpretation of the pattern rather than a direct test
- The status-as-signal function may be partially *legitimate*: readers with limited reading time rationally use author reputation as a filter, so the resulting citation boost is not purely distortion
- Evidence is from biomedical science using HHMI as the status shock; fields with different norms or status signals (e.g., mathematics, economics) may show different patterns

## Other Notes
This claim reframes status-based citation inflation from noise to a *rational collective heuristic* gone wrong: the system functions well for high-signal, within-field work and misfires most for the work society most needs to discover (novelty, interdisciplinarity). The policy implication is that status corrections matter most at the frontier. `[~T]`
