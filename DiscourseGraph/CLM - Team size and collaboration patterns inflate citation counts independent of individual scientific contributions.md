## Grounding
> "credit is collectively determined by the scientific community, not by individual coauthors. Indeed, individual team-members are often left helpless when their true contribution differs from what is perceived by the community." (Wang & Barabási 2021)

Citation counts accumulate at the paper level but are attributed to individuals as if contributions were separable and credit were additive. They are neither. A team-authored paper receives more citations on average than a solo paper regardless of the individual contribution of any given author; a scientist embedded in large, high-profile collaborations will accumulate citations faster than a similarly talented solo investigator. Simultaneously, credit is allocated disproportionately to eminent contributors regardless of actual task contribution, and gender, institutional affiliation, and review process blind conditions all introduce systematic biases in whose work gets read, accepted, and cited.

**Supporting evidence:**
- [[EVD - Team-authored papers 6.3 times more likely than solo papers to reach 1000 citations - @wangBarabasiScienceScience2021]] — raw team-size advantage in citation accumulation, holding quality constant
- [[EVD - Disruption drops as team size grows from 1 to 50 while citation counts increase - @wangBarabasiScienceScience2021]] — citations and scientific novelty move in *opposite* directions with team size; citations do not track disruptiveness
- [[EVD - Female economists receive zero tenure credit for papers coauthored with male economists - @wangBarabasiScienceScience2021]] — same paper generates differential career benefit by gender; citation-generating work is attributed asymmetrically
- [[EVD - WSDM 2017 randomized experiment shows well-known authors 63pct more likely accepted under single-blind review - @wangBarabasiScienceScience2021]] — upstream of citation counts: author identity directly causes differential acceptance, propagating status into the citation record

**Key caveats:**
- Team papers citing more is partly because teams genuinely do more impactful or comprehensive work (resource pooling, complementary expertise); the full gap is not purely a confound
- The gender credit gap may reflect unmeasured contribution differences in some fraction of cases, not only bias
- The WSDM experiment is from one CS conference; generalizability to all science, and to citation patterns (beyond acceptance), requires additional evidence

## Other Notes
This is the broadest structural argument against relying on individual citation counts: even before the Matthew effect operates, team composition and review process create systematic citation advantages unrelated to individual contribution. Addressing this confound may require both better credit attribution methods and review process reforms, not just citation normalization. `[~T]`
