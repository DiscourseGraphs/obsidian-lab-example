Analysis of top-30 US economics faculty found that female economists who collaborated with one or more male economists received essentially zero improvement in tenure prospects from those papers, while male economists received equal credit for solo and collaborative work.

## Description

> "When a female economist collaborates with one or more male economists, her tenure prospects don't improve at all. In other words, when it comes to tenure, women get essentially zero credit if they collaborate with men." ([[Wang & Barabási - 2021 - The Science of Science|p. ]])

The same citation-generating publication confers differential career benefit depending on the gender composition of the authorship team. A co-authored paper adds to the citation count of both the female and male author equally, but it advances the tenure prospects of only the male author. `[~T]` This is evidence that citation counts, while symmetric in their accumulation, are interpreted asymmetrically in evaluation — and that this asymmetry is systematically gendered. `[~T]` It also means that female economists who work in teams to be competitive in publication volume receive less return per citation than their male counterparts. `[~T]`

## Methods Context

### What?
Analysis of tenure outcomes for economists at top-30 US economics departments (N=552) as a function of publication record and coauthor gender composition.

### How?
Regression of tenure outcome on number of solo publications, number of co-authored publications, and interaction of co-authorship with gender; comparisons between male and female faculty.

### Who?
552 faculty at top-30 US economics departments, tracked over career trajectories including tenure decisions.

## Other Notes
- The study is specific to economics, where alphabetical authorship ordering may amplify the effect — in sociology, where contribution-based ordering is the norm, the bias is reportedly smaller.
- Demonstrates that citations are not a gender-neutral metric: the same citation stock maps to different professional rewards depending on who produced it and with whom.
- Relevant not only to the QUE but to the broader debate about whether citation count reforms (e.g., fractional attribution) or authorship convention reforms (contribution-based ordering) are the right policy lever.
