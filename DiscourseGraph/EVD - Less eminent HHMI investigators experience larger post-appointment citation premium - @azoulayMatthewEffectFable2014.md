HHMI investigators with fewer cumulative citations at appointment and those who were younger at appointment experienced a larger post-appointment citation boost to their pre-existing articles than more eminent or older investigators.

## Description

> "We find that the articles published by (relatively) less eminent scientists benefit more from the status shock." ([[Azoulay et al. - 2014 - Matthew Effect or Fable.pdf#page=26|p. 26]])

> "Column 6 in Table 2 interacts this variable with the treatment effect. As anticipated, the citation premium is larger in magnitude for younger investigators." ([[Azoulay et al. - 2014 - Matthew Effect or Fable.pdf#page=24|p. 24]])

The diminishing-returns pattern is consistent with the uncertainty mechanism: a status jump conveys more new information about scientists whose reputations are less well established. `[~T]` For a researcher already in the top decile of citations, HHMI appointment adds little to what the community already knows; for a less-eminent early-career scientist, the same prize conveys a large upward signal. `[~T]` This has implications for citation-based comparisons across career stages: status-driven citation premiums are larger and more distorting for junior than senior researchers. `[~T]`

### How?
Regression models adding interaction terms HHMI × pre-appointment citation quartile and HHMI × age at appointment, within the matched HHMI-ECPW article pair framework with article-level quality controls.

## Other Notes
- There are diminishing returns to status shocks at the apex of the status hierarchy: the HHMI prize, already a signal of elite standing, adds less to the citation prospects of already-eminent scientists.
- This finding complicates cross-career-stage citation comparisons: junior scientists face larger status-driven distortions in their citation counts than senior ones, making simple normalization by career stage insufficient.
