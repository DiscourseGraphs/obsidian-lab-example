Analysis of 124 Nobel laureates showed that publication of a major discovery boosted citation rates for the author's previously published papers, including papers not topically related to the discovery.

## Description

> "Analyses of 124 Nobel laureates show that this boost is common: The publication of a major discovery increases the citation rates of papers the author published before. Interestingly, the older papers that enjoyed the citation boosts are not necessarily related to the topic of the new discovery." ([[Wang & Barabási - 2021 - The Science of Science|p. ]])

When a scientist publishes a breakthrough, the scientific community retroactively revisits their earlier work — including work on unrelated topics. This cannot be explained by intellectual content: a major discovery in, say, protein folding does not make older papers in membrane biology scientifically more relevant. `[~T]` The citation boost to unrelated prior papers is therefore pure status effect: author identity propagates upward citation pressure across a portfolio regardless of content. `[~T]` It also implies that a scientist's total citation count reflects not just their work's quality but the timing and clustering of their breakthroughs. `[~T]`

## Methods Context

### What?
Retrospective citation analysis of pre-breakthrough papers by Nobel laureates, comparing citation rates before and after the publication of the prize-winning discovery, for topically related and unrelated prior papers separately.

### How?
Event study design around the publication date of the major discovery; classification of prior papers as topically related or unrelated based on content similarity.

### Who?
124 Nobel laureates across science disciplines.

## Other Notes
- The spread of the citation boost to *unrelated* papers is the critical detail: if only related papers were boosted, one could argue it reflects intellectual influence (citing the background for the breakthrough). The unrelated-paper boost isolates the pure author-identity channel.
- This effect is distinct from the HHMI pre-appointment boost (which covers existing papers before a status shock) but operates through the same mechanism: status reassessment causes readers to (re)visit a scientist's portfolio beyond what content would predict.
- Implication for career-level citation counts: a scientist who publishes a late-career breakthrough will have an inflated total citation count for all prior work, not just the breakthrough — making cross-career-stage comparisons unreliable.
