Following the death of a scientific superstar, collaborators experience a lasting 5–10% decline in quality-adjusted publication rates; the effect is larger (10.5%) for sudden unexpected deaths and smaller (4.7%) for anticipated deaths, and shows no evidence of recovery.

## Description

> "We estimate the magnitude of spillovers generated by 161 academic 'superstars' onto their collaborators' research output. These life scientists died while still being actively engaged in science, thus providing an exogenous source of variation in the structure of their collaborators' coauthorship networks. Following the death of a superstar, we find that collaborators experience, on average, a lasting 5 to 10% decline in their quality-adjusted publication rates." ([[Azoulay et al. - NBER WORKING PAPER SERIES.pdf#page=2|p. 2]])

Key statistics from Table 5, Panel A (column 1a):
- Total quality-adjusted publication output: **7.4% decrease** (significant)
- Sudden/unanticipated death: **~10.5% decrease**
- Anticipated death: **~4.7% decrease**

> "we find no evidence of recovery — the effect of superstar extinction appears permanent." ([[Azoulay et al. - NBER WORKING PAPER SERIES.pdf#page=24|p. 24]])

Quality adjustment: publications weighted by Journal Impact Factor; for the elite subsample, article-level citation percentiles (within-vintage distribution) are also used.

> "The magnitude of the effect remains in the 3.5% to 6.1% range, whether we examine the effect of superstar extinction on raw number of publications, publications that fall above the median number of citations, publications in the top quartile, or publications in the top ventile. The magnitude of the effect increases to 10% when focusing on 'blockbuster' publications — those falling in the top percentile of the citation distribution." ([[Azoulay et al. - NBER WORKING PAPER SERIES.pdf#page=25|p. 25]])

## Methods Context

### What?
Quality-adjusted publication output (JIF-weighted publications; also article-level citation percentiles for elite subsample). NIH grant outcomes as secondary measure.

### How?
Fixed-effect Poisson quasi-maximum likelihood estimation on a panel of 8,220 collaborator-star dyads. Treatment = superstar death (exogenous shock). Standard errors clustered at superstar level. Difference-in-differences with matched control superstars to account for age and calendar time trends.

### Who?
161 eminent biomedical scientists who died while actively engaged in science (1975–2006), identified via 7 criteria (top-5% NIH funding, ISI Highly Cited, top patenter, NAS member, MERIT awardee, HHMI investigator, early-career prize winner). 8,220 collaborator-star dyads from AAMC Faculty Roster.

## Other Notes
- > "At the very least, these results suggest that superstar exposure is not limited to the production of relatively less significant scientific knowledge." ([[Azoulay et al. - NBER WORKING PAPER SERIES.pdf#page=25|p. 25]])
- This EVD is relevant to the research question obliquely: it shows that citations (used here as a quality-adjustment weight) do track something real — the knowledge spillovers from highly-cited scientists are genuine, not purely status-driven.
- The permanence of the effect argues against a "bereavement" or purely social/network explanation.
